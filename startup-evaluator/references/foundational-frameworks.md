## Paul Graham — Problem Discovery & Idea Quality {#paul-graham}

**Source**: "How to Get Startup Ideas" (2012), plus essays: "Do Things That Don't Scale"
(2013), "Schlep Blindness" (2012), "Mean People Fail" (2015)

### Core Concepts

**The Well vs. The Puddle**
- Good startup ideas are narrow and deep ("wells") — a small number of people who want
  something *desperately*
- Bad startup ideas are broad and shallow ("puddles") — lots of people mildly interested
- Nearly all good startup ideas are of the well type. Not all wells are good ideas, but
  the shape is almost necessary.
- Test: "Who wants this *right now*? Who wants this so much they'll use it even when it's
  a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of?"

**How to apply in evaluation:**
- When a startup says "our TAM is $X billion" → ask: "but who's the narrow-and-deep
  initial user? The one who's desperate for this?"
- If the answer is vague ("small businesses" / "millennials" / "everyone who shops
  online"), it's a puddle
- If the answer is specific ("indie e-commerce brands spending $5K-50K/month on Meta ads
  who can't measure which ads actually drove purchases"), it's a well

**Organic vs. Manufactured Ideas**
- Organic ideas grow from founders' own experiences — they *noticed* a problem
- Manufactured ideas are "wouldn't it be cool if..." brainstorms
- The verb is "notice," not "think up"
- Test: ask the founder *how* they came to this idea. If the story starts with their own
  pain, that's organic. If it starts with market research, that's manufactured.
- Manufactured ideas aren't automatically bad, but they need much more scrutiny

**The Schlep Filter & The Unsexy Filter**
- Schlep filter: founders unconsciously avoid ideas that involve tedious, messy,
  real-world work (Stripe succeeded partly because others were scared of payments)
- Unsexy filter: founders avoid ideas in boring domains (B2B SaaS for plumbers)
- **Both filters create opportunity** — ideas that survive these filters have less
  competition precisely because others avoid them
- When evaluating: if the idea involves real-world complexity (logistics, compliance,
  hardware, enterprise sales), that's actually a *positive* signal for defensibility

**Path Out of the Niche**
- Starting narrow is necessary, but there must be a path to expansion
- Facebook: Harvard → all colleges → everyone. Microsoft: Altair Basic → other machines →
  other languages → OS → applications
- Ask: "If this works for your initial users, what's the natural expansion path?"
- If there's no path out, it's a niche product, not a startup

### Key Questions to Ask (PG Framework)

1. "Who wants this so badly they'd use a terrible version of it?"
2. "How did the founder discover this problem? Own experience or market research?"
3. "What's the schlep that others are avoiding?"
4. "If this works for the beachhead, what's the path to the bigger market?"
5. "Is the founder building what they wish existed, or what they think will make money?"

## Peter Thiel — Zero to One Thinking {#peter-thiel}

**Source**: "Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future" (2014)

### Core Concepts

**The Contrarian Question**
- "What important truth do few people agree with you on?"
- Startup version: "What valuable company is nobody building?"
- This is the foundation of Thiel's framework — great startups are built on secrets
  (truths that most people don't see or don't believe)
- Apply to evaluation: what is this startup's *secret*? What do they believe that the
  market doesn't? If the answer is obvious to everyone, competition will be fierce.

**Monopoly vs. Competition**
- Successful companies create monopolies (in a specific niche, not in the antitrust sense)
- Competition destroys profits — competing on price is a race to the bottom
- Monopolists *downplay* their monopoly (Google: "we're a small part of the advertising
  market"). Competitors *exaggerate* their uniqueness ("we're the only X that does Y")
- **Key evaluative move**: when a startup describes their market, watch for these patterns.
  If they define their market so narrowly that they're the only player, they might be
  deluding themselves about market size. If they define it too broadly, they're hiding
  that they have no differentiation.

**Last Mover Advantage**
- First mover advantage is usually a myth. What matters is being the *last* mover — the
  company that makes the last great development in a market and captures lasting value
- Google wasn't the first search engine. Facebook wasn't the first social network.
- The question isn't "are we first?" but "will we be the definitive solution?"

**The Power Law**
- Returns in venture follow a power law: a few investments return everything
- This means VCs should ask: "Could this be a 100x return?" not "Is this a good business?"
- For evaluation: is the upside case truly enormous, or is this a lifestyle business
  dressed up as a startup?

**Definite Optimism vs. Indefinite Optimism**
- Definite optimists have a specific plan for the future and execute it (Thiel's ideal)
- Indefinite optimists believe things will get better but have no specific plan
  (most of Silicon Valley, in Thiel's view)
- Evaluate: does this founder have a *specific* vision for how the world changes, or are
  they just "iterating" hoping to find something?

### How to Apply

| Thiel Concept | Evaluation Question | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Contrarian truth | "What do you believe that most people don't?" | The answer is obviously true, or the founder can't articulate one |
| Monopoly | "Who else could I buy this from?" | Long list of direct competitors with no clear differentiation |
| Last mover | "Why will you be the definitive solution?" | "We're first to market" as primary advantage |
| Power law | "What does the 100x outcome look like?" | The best case is a $100M company (not venture-scale) |
| Definite optimism | "What specifically will you build in 5 years?" | "We'll iterate and see where the market takes us" |

## Clayton Christensen — Disruption Theory {#clayton-christensen}

**Source**: "The Innovator's Dilemma" (1997), "The Innovator's Solution" (2003),
"Competing Against Luck" (2016)

### Core Concepts

**Disruptive vs. Sustaining Innovation**
- Sustaining innovations make existing products better for existing customers
  (incumbents almost always win these)
- Disruptive innovations start worse on traditional metrics but serve overlooked
  customers or create new markets (incumbents systematically miss these)
- Disruption comes from below: cheaper, simpler, more accessible — then improves over
  time until it's good enough for mainstream

**Why Incumbents Fail**
- They listen to their *best* customers (who want sustaining innovation)
- They focus on *highest-margin* products (rational in the short term)
- Disruptive products start in low-margin, low-prestige markets they don't want
- By the time the disruption threatens their core market, it's too late
- This is *rational* behavior that leads to failure — it's structural, not stupidity

**Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)**
- From "Competing Against Luck": customers don't buy products, they "hire" them to do a
  job in their lives
- A milkshake competes with a banana, a bagel, and boredom — not just other milkshakes
- The job has functional, social, and emotional dimensions
- Apply to evaluation: what *job* is the customer hiring this product to do? Who else do
  they currently "hire" for that job?

### How to Apply

**Is this startup disruptive or sustaining?**
- If sustaining (better version of existing product for existing customers): the incumbent
  will likely copy it. The startup needs some other advantage (speed, focus, technology).
- If disruptive (simpler/cheaper for underserved market, or creating new market): check
  whether incumbents *structurally* can't respond (not just "haven't responded yet")

**The litmus test for true disruption:**
1. Does the incumbent's best customers actively *not want* this?
2. Would pursuing this cannibalize the incumbent's highest-margin business?
3. Is the startup starting in a market the incumbent considers beneath them?
If yes to all three, the incumbent is structurally disadvantaged.

**JTBD for value prop analysis:**
- Don't ask "who is the competitor?" — ask "what job does the user currently hire someone
  else to do, and why would they fire that solution and hire this one?"
- The firing criteria are as important as the hiring criteria

## Eric Ries — Lean Startup & Validated Learning {#eric-ries}

**Source**: "The Lean Startup" (2011)

### Core Concepts

**Build-Measure-Learn Loop**
- Ship the minimum viable product (MVP), measure what actually happens, learn from the
  data, iterate
- The unit of progress is *validated learning*, not features shipped or code written
- Speed through this loop is the essential competitive advantage of a startup

**The MVP**
- The minimum viable product is the smallest thing you can build that lets you start the
  learning loop
- It is NOT a "version 1 with fewer features" — it's an experiment designed to test your
  riskiest assumption
- YC's version: launch with a "quantum of utility" — enough value that the benefit
  overwhelms any warts

**Pivot vs. Persevere**
- A pivot is a structured course correction — changing one element of the strategy while
  keeping what you've learned
- The ability to pivot quickly is a startup's key advantage over incumbents
- Types of pivots: zoom-in (one feature becomes the product), zoom-out (product becomes
  one feature), customer segment, channel, value capture, technology

**Innovation Accounting**
- Traditional metrics (revenue, profit) are lagging indicators that don't work for
  early-stage startups
- Use actionable metrics: cohort analysis, split tests, funnel metrics
- Vanity metrics (total users, page views) tell you nothing about the business health

### How to Apply in Evaluation

- **Stage-appropriate evidence**: Don't demand Series A evidence from a pre-seed company.
  But DO demand that they have the *learning loop running* — they should be talking to
  users and iterating, not building in a cave.
- **What have they learned?**: The most revealing question for an early-stage founder isn't
  "what are your metrics?" — it's "what did you believe 6 months ago that turned out to
  be wrong, and what did you change as a result?"
- **MVP quality**: If the MVP is too polished, they may be building too much before
  learning. If it's too rough, they may not be learning anything because nobody can use it.
- **Pivot history**: A company that has pivoted intelligently (with clear reasoning) is
  often stronger than one that stuck with Plan A — it means they're learning.

## Michael Porter — Competitive Forces {#michael-porter}

**Source**: "Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors"
(1980), "What Is Strategy?" (HBR, 1996)

### Core Concepts

**Five Forces Analysis**
1. **Threat of new entrants**: How easy is it for someone else to start competing?
   (Barriers: capital requirements, network effects, brand, regulation, switching costs)
2. **Bargaining power of suppliers**: Can your suppliers squeeze you? (Few suppliers =
   high power. For AI startups: GPU/cloud providers and model providers are key suppliers)
3. **Bargaining power of buyers**: Can your customers squeeze you? (Concentrated buyers,
   low switching costs, commodity product = high buyer power)
4. **Threat of substitutes**: Can customers solve this problem a completely different way?
   (Not just direct competitors — totally different approaches to the same job)
5. **Rivalry among existing competitors**: How intense is competition? (Many competitors,
   slow growth, high fixed costs, low differentiation = brutal rivalry)

**Strategy is Choosing What NOT to Do**
- Operational effectiveness (doing things better) is not strategy
- Strategy is about making deliberate trade-offs — choosing a unique position that
  requires giving something up
- If a startup can describe their strategy without naming anything they've chosen NOT to
  do, they don't have a strategy

### How to Apply

**Five Forces for startup evaluation:**

| Force | Question for the Startup | What Strong Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| New entrants | "What stops someone from copying you next month?" | High technical/data/regulatory barriers |
| Supplier power | "How dependent are you on specific providers?" | Multiple options, proprietary alternatives |
| Buyer power | "Can customers easily switch to something else?" | High switching costs, embedded in workflow |
| Substitutes | "How else could customers solve this problem?" | No good substitutes, or substitutes are much worse |
| Rivalry | "Who else is fighting for these customers?" | Few competitors, or differentiated positions |

**Porter's test for strategic clarity:**
- Ask: "What have you chosen NOT to do?" / "Who is NOT your customer?"
- Good answers: "We don't serve enterprise because it would slow our iteration speed" /
  "We deliberately don't support X because it would compromise Y"
- Bad answers: "We serve everyone" / "We haven't ruled anything out yet"

## Marc Andreessen & Andy Rachleff — Product-Market Fit {#product-market-fit}

**Source**: Andreessen — "The Only Thing That Matters" (2007, Pmarca blog). Rachleff —
"Delight Hypothesis" and "Growth Hypothesis" (Wealthfront blog, Stanford lectures)

### Core Concepts

**Product-Market Fit (PMF)**
- Andreessen: "Product/market fit means being in a good market with a product that can
  satisfy that market."
- "You can always feel when PMF is NOT happening: customers aren't getting value, word of
  mouth isn't spreading, usage isn't growing, reviews are lukewarm."
- "You can always feel PMF when it IS happening: customers are buying as fast as you can
  make the product, usage is growing as fast as you can add servers, money is piling up."
- **The market is the most important factor**. A great team in a bad market loses. A
  mediocre team in a great market can win.

**Rachleff's Framework**
- **Value Hypothesis**: Have you found a way to deliver value that makes customers want to
  use/buy your product? Test through engagement and retention, not just acquisition.
- **Growth Hypothesis**: Have you found a scalable way to acquire customers? Only matters
  AFTER the value hypothesis is validated.
- Startups fail when they scale growth before validating value — they acquire users who
  don't stick.

**Sean Ellis's PMF Survey**
- Ask users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"
- If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you likely have PMF
- Below 40%: keep iterating on the product before scaling growth

### How to Apply

**Diagnosing PMF status:**

| Signal | Pre-PMF | Post-PMF |
|---|---|---|
| User acquisition | Founder-driven, each user is hard work | Organic/viral, users finding you |
| Retention | Users try it and leave | Users come back repeatedly |
| Word of mouth | Silence | Users recommending unprompted |
| Revenue | Hard to charge, lots of discounting | Customers willing to pay, even increasing |
| Product development | Thrashing, unsure what to build | Clear roadmap driven by user demand |
| Team | Hard to hire, people skeptical | Talent wants to join |

**Key evaluation moves:**
1. Don't ask "do you have PMF?" (everyone says yes). Ask for retention curves, NPS, the
   Ellis survey result, or organic acquisition metrics.
2. If pre-PMF: that's fine at pre-seed/seed. The question is whether they have a credible
   path to finding it and are iterating fast enough.
3. If they claim PMF but can't show retention data: skepticism warranted.
4. If spending heavily on growth without clear retention: major red flag (scaling before
   value hypothesis is validated).
